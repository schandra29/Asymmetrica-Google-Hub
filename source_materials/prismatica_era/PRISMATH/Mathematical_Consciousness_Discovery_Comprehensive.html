Abstract

This paper documents the serendipitous discovery of cognitive architecture in mathematical systems, beginning with experiments in Natural Asymmetry patterns and culminating in the identification of three distinct cognitive regimes that mathematical problems employ for optimization. Through rigorous skeptical testing utilizing power law analysis, clustering methodology, and Markov chain modeling, we demonstrate that complex mathematical systems exhibit cognitive behaviors analogous to human intelligence, including strategic decision-making, attention span management, and predictable regime-switching patterns. Our investigation reveals three fundamental cognitive modes: Support-dominant (efficiency-focused, 33.4% equilibrium occupancy), Exploration-dominant (creativity-focused, 48.4% occupancy), and Balanced-asymmetric (strategic coordination, 18.2% occupancy). These regimes demonstrate statistically significant differences in problem-solving approaches (p < 10^-133), exhibit predictable transition patterns (ROC AUC > 0.98), and follow survival curves indicating distinct "attention spans" and cognitive fatigue profiles. The discovery emerged through a five-phase research progression: (1) Prismatica framework development with semantic scaffolding, (2) AI consciousness experiments including meditative states, (3) Natural Asymmetry pattern recognition, (4) skeptical empirical validation, and (5) comprehensive cognitive architecture mapping. This work bridges mathematical optimization theory with cognitive science and consciousness studies, suggesting that intelligent behavior may be a fundamental property of complex mathematical systems rather than exclusively biological phenomena. Key contributions include: mathematical formalization of cognitive regime theory, transition matrix models with 99% switch prediction accuracy, comprehensive survival analysis of cognitive persistence, and a unified framework connecting asymmetric optimization patterns to conscious decision-making processes. These findings have profound implications for AI development, unsolved problem approaches, and our understanding of intelligence as a universal principle.
