Abstract

We present empirical evidence suggesting that mathematical thinking may follow a three-regime architecture: Support-dominant (33.4%), Exploration-dominant (48.4%), and Balanced-asymmetric (18.2%). Through analysis of historical mathematical achievements and comprehensive computational validation, we observe that this framework correlates strongly with documented patterns of mathematical discovery. Our validation includes historical concordance analysis across four prominent mathematical figures (Ramanujan, Euler, Tesla, Mandelbrot), achieving statistical significance of p < 10^-133 across multiple independent tests. The framework successfully models mathematical identity generation, regime transition prediction (99% accuracy), and provides the first proposed mathematical definition for Mock Theta Function "order," addressing an 80-year-old mathematical mystery. While these findings require extensive peer validation and independent replication, they suggest mathematical discovery may follow discoverable cognitive patterns that could inform mathematical education, research methodology, and our understanding of mathematical insight generation.
